Data with less technology and more personality

This is a work-in-progress to document my experiments at doing the opposite of big data; I explore comparatively low-tech techniques to document my own life as a path towards surprising, actionable insights.

I've drawn inspiration from numerous sources: the data-filled personal dashboards of Nicholas Felton, the elegant Bullet Journal method of Ryder Carroll, and the fascinating explorations of human nature by Christian Rudder.

After a PhD in Applied Physics at Harvard (with a detour through the intersection of science and cooking), I now work on the Business Intelligence Infrastucture team at Fitbit, which has been a crash course into dealing with large data sets. Even before my current job, I was seeking ways to use my data from their trackers to find patterns in my daily activity.

Disclaimer: although I spend my days analyzing data at Fitbit, all the views here are my own.

 What I do

I think of my job as being a sous chef for data, as I help turn raw numbers into dashboards with actionable information.

Departments like Research & Department provide the ingredients, my team processes them, and sends them out to the "front of the house" as dashboards. This metaphor leads to the following lessons I've learned:

  1. Have a sense of urgency: just like how your efficiency with a knife limits your speed in the kitchen, your effieciency at choosing the right tools, writing efficient queries, and rapidly communicating your results limits the number of projects you can take on.
  2. Taste everything: in this case, the equivalent of tasting is checking for duplicates, missing records, and other mistakes that could ruin the final product.
  3. Track everything: just like how a well-run kitchen knows their exact food costs and the cooking times for each dish, knowing the time and resources required for each data processing job is essential.

 Tryo

A tryo is a one- to four-week long trial of a new habit to see if it's a good fit. You can pick whichever analogy appeals to you more:

  • Your daily routine is like the master branch of a git repository. Each time you change some part of that is a routine. Some are bad ideas that are stashed away; others are successful and get pulled back into the main branch.
  • Creating your daily routine is like dating different habits. It's difficult to try too many habits at once and some habits just aren't meant for you. However, even if you don't end up incorporating a habit for life, it can change the way you think about the world.

 Kitchen Experiments

Since I no longer work in a science lab, my kitchen has become my venue for experimentation. I'm especially interested in recipes that challenge assumptions with their simplicity (i.e. pea soup = pea + water + salt) or their unusual use of ingredients (e.g. muffin = almond flour + egg).
